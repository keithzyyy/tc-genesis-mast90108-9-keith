What this research does
With 149 candidate predictors and 2¹⁴⁹ possible models, finding which environmental variables best predict tropical cyclone formation is a needle-in-a-haystack problem. This thesis proposes a smarter search strategy using MCMC.

Why not just use standard methods?
Exhaustive searchChecks all 2¹⁴⁹ submodels — completely infeasible
StepwiseFast but greedy — gets trapped in local optima, doesn't converge to true model
LASSOArbitrarily drops correlated variables, unstable variable sets
MCMC random searchPrincipled, scalable, avoids local optima — this paper's approach
Core insight: Construct a probability distribution over models where lower-BIC models receive higher probability mass. An MCMC chain then naturally spends more time near the best models — without ever enumerating them all. The BIC minimiser tends to be both the lowest-BIC model and the most frequently visited.
Why BIC over AIC? Unlike AIC, BIC asymptotically selects not just a correctly specified model but the most parsimonious one — penalising with ½ · p · log(n) rather than p. This matters when p is large and parsimony is the goal.
How the random search works
Each model is encoded as a binary vector v ∈ {0,1}¹⁴⁹, where vⱼ = 1 means variable j is included. The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm samples these vectors proportional to exp(−λ · BIC).
1
Initialise four overdispersed chains
Full model (all 149 vars), empty model, sparse (5% per var), random (50% per var) — ensures the search doesn't depend on a single starting point.
2
Propose a neighbour by flipping one bit
At each step, randomly add or remove exactly one variable. This "one-bit flip" kernel is symmetric — the proposal ratio cancels in the acceptance probability.
3
Accept or reject via Metropolis rule
If proposed BIC is lower → always accept. If higher → accept with probability exp(−λ · ΔBIC). This allows occasional uphill moves to escape local minima.
4
Discard burn-in, record samples
First 1,000 of 41,000 steps discarded. Each remaining step records the current model. 4 chains × 12 λ values = ~1.97M model evaluations, each requiring a full logistic regression fit.
5
Extract BIC minimiser and elite set
The model with lowest BIC is the primary result. All models within ΔBIC ≤ 2 of this minimum form the "elite set" — statistically indistinguishable by Bayes Factor interpretation (Kass & Raftery 1995).

Key convergence finding: The Gibbs sampler (which updates all 149 variables per iteration) showed non-stationarity after 9 days of compute — attributable to extreme predictor collinearity. The MH algorithm was used instead, achieving near-convergence at λ = 0.75 with R̂ = 1.013.

Simulation study
33 synthetic scenarios varying sample size (n), predictor count (p), signal-to-noise ratio, and correlation. Ground truth variables known in advance — performance measured by recovery rate.
Fundamental flaw in the SNR definition: Signal-to-noise ratio was defined via Wald and LRT p-value bands (e.g. "moderate" = p ∈ [0.01, 0.05)). But BIC uses the same underlying likelihood to decide inclusion. This creates circularity — the SNR definition is criterion-dependent, not criterion-agnostic. A variable deliberately set to "moderate" signal may be correctly excluded by BIC (because z² < log n), making it impossible to distinguish a genuine procedure failure from a correctly functioning penalty. The thesis acknowledges this and proposes McKelvey-Zavoina pseudo-R² as a more principled future alternative.

Weak SNRBIC correctly penalises — not necessarily a procedure failure
Weak-signal variables were excluded in 13 of 33 scenarios. In 8 of those 13, the true model still appeared in the top 5 BIC minimisers within ΔBIC ≤ 2 — statistically indistinguishable from the selected model. Because SNR is defined via the same likelihood BIC uses, it is difficult to know whether exclusions reflect a procedure failure or a correctly functioning penalty.

Why 54 variables at 12h lead? The dataset measures the same physical phenomena at 9 pressure levels — creating many variables that are semantically the same driver at different altitudes. At longer lead times, this redundancy collapses to fewer dominant predictors (Wijnands et al. found 4 dominant variables at 12–24h).
Why keep correlated variables?
The final model contains highly correlated variable pairs — some with Pearson r > 0.99. The natural concern is whether these inclusions are spurious. Systematic ablation testing says they are not.
The concern: Standard statistical intuition says correlated predictors are redundant — one should be dropped. LASSO will drop all but one from a correlated group. So why does the random search keep both geopotential at 925 hPa and mean sea level pressure (r = 0.993)?
The answer: High pairwise correlation does not imply identical conditional effects. Each variable may encode distinct information once the other predictors are controlled for. The evidence is empirical: systematically removing each correlated variable from the final model causes large BIC increases and predictive degradation — confirming distinct contributions.

Geopotential at adjacent pressure levels can correlate at r > 0.98, yet the thesis shows graphically (Figure 1) that the relationship between geopotential and TC genesis differs meaningfully across levels — genesis is more prevalent at large geopotential at low pressure levels (200–300 hPa) but not at higher pressure values. This visual pattern supports the inclusion of multiple levels as capturing distinct physical signals from different atmospheric strata.
Residual concern: Ablation tests are pairwise — removing one variable at a time. They do not rule out the possibility that a different, simpler subset of variables could achieve the same BIC with fewer correlated predictors. The thesis acknowledges this and proposes hierarchical feature-grouping as future work to address it.
Random search vs LASSO
LASSO is the standard high-dimensional regularisation benchmark. Both methods achieve comparable predictive performance, but differ fundamentally in how they handle correlated predictors and model size.
MCMC random search
Samples from a BIC-induced distribution over models. Finds globally near-optimal subsets without penalising coefficients — pure subset selection.

Limitations & future work
The thesis is candid about its constraints. Several limitations are methodological; others are practical. Each points toward concrete future improvements.
Key limitations
Circular SNR definition (fundamental flaw). Signal-to-noise ratio was operationalised via Wald and LRT p-value bands. Because BIC uses the same likelihood to decide inclusion, the SNR definition is criterion-dependent — a variable deliberately set to "moderate signal" may be correctly excluded by BIC, making it impossible to objectively evaluate whether exclusions reflect a procedure failure or a correctly functioning penalty. McKelvey-Zavoina pseudo-R² is proposed as a criterion-agnostic alternative.
Gibbs sampler non-convergence. The Gibbs implementation (updating all 149 variables per sweep) failed to converge after 9 days of HPC compute. Strong predictor collinearity — not high dimensionality per se — is the most likely cause, as the synthetic high-dimensional (p=160) case with controlled correlation converged in < 1 day.
λ selection is post-hoc and empirical. The optimal λ = 0.75 was determined by running the full grid and identifying where the BIC minimum occurred. There is no principled rule for selecting λ upfront. The thesis proposes future work to derive empirical rules from short pilot runs.
No external validation set. The final model was selected using 5-fold CV on the same dataset used for the random search — there was no held-out test set reserved from the start. The CV performance is a consistency check, not a true generalisation estimate.
Conservative correlation design in simulation. The factorial study only controlled pairwise correlations (between X1 and X2). Real high-dimensional settings often involve clusters of correlated variables — a richer design would introduce "scale of collinearity" as a separate factor. Enforcing multiple high correlations simultaneously risks non-positive semi-definite covariance matrices.
Large final model dimension. 54 variables is large by traditional standards. The thesis argues this reflects genuine multi-level physical structure at 12h lead time (consistent with Wijnands et al.), but cannot rule out that a sparser model with equivalent BIC exists but was not sampled.
Single lead time. Results apply only to the 12-hour TC genesis prediction window. Wijnands et al. showed that dominant predictors shift meaningfully at longer lead times — the procedure would need to be rerun for other windows.
Proposed future work
1
Principled SNR via McKelvey-Zavoina pseudo-R²
Replace p-value bands with a criterion-agnostic measure: R² = β^T Σβ / (β^T Σβ + π²/3). Solves the fundamental circularity in the simulation study design.
2
Hierarchical feature grouping
Run separate random searches across coarse (one rep per physical field), intermediate (2–3 macro pressure layers), and full resolution (all 36 shear pairs). Identifies which pressure-level groupings carry distinct vs redundant information.
3
Block Gibbs sampler
Group variables into blocks by physical family or observed correlation, then update one block at a time. Would address the slow mixing caused by collinearity, and could make Gibbs feasible on the TC genesis dataset.
4
Adaptive λ scheduling
Start with λ ≤ 1 for exploration, then increase to λ > 1 once promising regions are identified. Would combine global exploration with local exploitation without requiring a full λ grid search.
5
Empirical λ-selection rules from short runs
Derive rules to select λ from 1,000–5,000 iteration pilot runs — reducing compute burden for practitioners who want to apply the method without running a full 12-value λ grid.
6
Model averaging for prediction
Use Akaike weights across near-optimal models for a weighted-average prediction. Extends the random search from variable selection to a full predictive ensemble.
7
Richer collinearity design in simulation
Introduce "number of correlated clusters" as a simulation factor, alongside methods to construct valid positive semi-definite covariance matrices under complex correlation structures.

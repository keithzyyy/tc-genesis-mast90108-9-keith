What this research does
With 149 candidate predictors and 2^149 possible models to consider, finding which environmental variables best predict tropical cyclone formation is a needle-in-a-haystack problem. This thesis proposes a smarter search.

Why not just use standard methods?
Exhaustive search Checks all 2^149 submodels — completely infeasible
Stepwise methods Fast but greedy — gets trapped in local minima
LASSO Arbitrarily drops correlated variables — unstable under high collinearity
MCMC random search Principled, scalable, avoids local optima — this paper's approach
Core insight: Instead of searching all models, construct a probability distribution over models where lower-BIC models get higher probability mass. An MCMC chain then naturally spends more time near the best models — without ever enumerating them all.
The model selection criterion used is BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion), chosen because — unlike AIC — it asymptotically selects not just a correct model but the most parsimonious one. The BIC penalises model complexity with a ½ · p · log(n) term, creating a meaningful "detection hurdle" for each additional variable.
How the random search works
Each candidate model is encoded as a binary vector v ∈ {0,1}^p, where vⱼ = 1 means variable j is included. The procedure samples these vectors from a probability distribution shaped by BIC.
The Metropolis-Hastings algorithm (used on TC data)
1
Start from an initial model
Four chains run in parallel with overdispersed starting points: full model (all 149 vars), empty model, sparse (5% chance per var), and random (50% chance per var).
2
Propose a neighbour model
Flip one random bit in the current binary vector — add or remove exactly one variable. This is the "one-bit flip" transition kernel.
3
Compute the acceptance probability
If the proposed model has a lower BIC → always accept. If higher BIC → accept with probability exp(−λ · ΔBIC). The λ parameter controls how willing the chain is to accept worse models.
4
Record and repeat
After 1,000 burn-in steps, each sampled model is recorded. With 41,000 steps per chain × 4 chains × 12 λ values = ~2 million model evaluations, each requiring a logistic regression fit.
5
Extract the BIC minimiser and elite set
The model with the lowest BIC across all samples is the primary result. All models within ΔBIC ≤ 2 of the minimum are "elite" — statistically indistinguishable by Bayes Factor interpretation.

Running marginal inclusion frequencies — should stabilise over iterations
Key result: At λ = 0.75, the rank-normalised R̂ = 1.013 — just above the 1.01 threshold, indicating near-convergence. The BIC minimiser at this λ also coincided with the second most frequently visited model in the sparse-start chain — strong evidence it represents a true optimum.
The λ (inverse-temperature) parameter
λ controls the sharpness of the probability distribution over models. Too cold (high λ): trapped in local minima. Too hot (low λ): random walk, poor exploitation. The sweet spot balances both.

Simulation study results
Before applying to real data, the procedure was stress-tested across 33 synthetic scenarios varying 4 factors. Ground truth variables were known in advance — performance was measured by how often the search recovered them.
Filter by SNR:
Strong SNR All ground truths recovered in all 5 correlation scenarios
With strong signals, all four ground truth variables (X1–X4) appeared with relative frequencies near 1.0 regardless of correlation. BIC minimiser was always a correct model.
Moderate SNR Mostly recovered — 1–2 scenarios missed a variable
Under moderate SNR with n=2000, p=16, the procedure correctly recovered all ground truths in 4 of 5 correlation levels. The single miss (corr = 0.5) was explained by BIC's log(n) hurdle — the variable's Wald z² was 7.07 < log(2000) = 7.6, so BIC correctly excluded it.
Weak SNR BIC correctly penalises — not a failure of the method
Weak-signal variables were often excluded by BIC even when they were genuine ground truths. This is by design — BIC requires evidence exceeding its log(n) hurdle. In 8 of 13 "failed" scenarios, the true model still appeared in the top 5 BIC minimisers within ΔBIC ≤ 2.
Relative frequency thresholds across SNR levels (n=2000, p=16, high correlation)
X1 and X3 maintain high frequencies across all SNR levels. X2 and X4 drop significantly under weak SNR.
Strong SNR Moderate SNR Weak SNR
Key finding: Using a 0.5 relative frequency cutoff identifies a near-correct model in all non-weak-SNR scenarios — missing at most one ground truth. The stronger variables (X1 and X3 by design) maintain high relative frequencies even under weak overall SNR.
TC genesis findings
41 "core variables" appear in all 38 near-optimal models. All six of Gray's (1975) canonical TC genesis drivers were recovered — confirming alignment with decades of meteorological literature.
Gray's 6 canonical drivers — all confirmed
Vertical wind shear (200–850 hPa)
Inverse of shear between lower and upper troposphere
p̂ = 1.00
Consistently negative coefficient across all 38 elite models — high wind shear inhibits TC formation by disrupting the vertical convective structure. The 200–850 hPa layer showed the strongest signal, with BIC increasing by 476 points when removed from the final model.
Low-level relative vorticity
Rotational motion of the atmosphere at lower levels
p̂ = 1.00
Relative vorticity at 200 hPa appears in all elite models with a consistently negative coefficient. This aligns with the physical mechanism: a pre-existing low-level rotational seed is necessary for TC organisation and spin-up.
Coriolis parameter
Earth's rotational effect — varies with latitude
p̂ = 1.00
The absolute Coriolis parameter appears in all elite models. TCs cannot form near the equator where the Coriolis force is too weak to sustain rotation — this is why they only develop beyond ~5° latitude.
Sea surface temperature
Ocean thermal energy source for TC development
p̂ = 1.00
SST has a consistently negative coefficient — counterintuitively, this reflects the model structure where other variables capture the direct positive effect, and SST's role is nuanced in the high-dimensional conditional context. The variable appears in all elite models, confirming its essential role.
Equivalent potential temperature diff
Surface to 500 hPa — measure of atmospheric instability
p̂ = 0.86
This variable captures thermodynamic instability — the larger the difference between surface and mid-troposphere equivalent potential temperature, the more energetically favourable TC development becomes. Present in 86% of elite models.
Mid-troposphere relative humidity
Moisture availability at 500–700 hPa
p̂ = 0.46
The weakest of Gray's six drivers in this analysis — relative humidity at 700 hPa appears in only 46% of sampled models. This lower frequency likely reflects collinearity with specific humidity and other moisture variables in the dataset, rather than physical unimportance.

Why does the model use 54 variables when Gray described only 6 drivers? The dataset measures the same physical phenomena at 9 pressure levels — creating many variables that are semantically the same driver at different altitudes. This is expected at a 12-hour lead time; Wijnands et al. found that at longer lead times the redundancy collapses to fewer dominant predictors.
